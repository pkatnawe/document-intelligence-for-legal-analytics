Experiments & Results

We didn't just assert which reader and model to use — we built an eval harness and measured it on a hand-verified gold set. This page is the evidence: the setup, the benchmark, what we learned, and how the results changed our architecture from "VLM over OCR" to a cheap, confidence-routed cascade.

What the experiments showed
  • On clean invoices, OCR-then-text beats VLM-direct — and is cheaper and faster. The "always use a VLM" instinct was wrong for clean documents.
  • The VLM's weakness was the model, not the approach. A stronger open VLM (Qwen3-VL-8B) lifted line-item F1 from 0.84 → 0.98 and read the hardest invoice perfectly.
  • A confidence-routed cascade wins overall — OCR for clean, VLM only for degraded scans — at near-OCR cost, firing the expensive path just 1 in 6 times.
  • All numbers are on a 3-invoice gold set — directional, not a verdict. The durable deliverable is the harness that keeps answering this as real data arrives.

The setup — a reproducible eval harness

Three small, reusable pieces (in eval/). The metric normalizes before comparing (currency symbols, date formats, money rounding, masked card digits) so a correct read isn't punished for formatting — and it doubles as a DSPy metric, so the same score can drive GEPA optimization later.

Experiment 1 — which reader × model? (clean invoices)

Every architecture run over the gold set, 3 trials, greedy decoding (temperature 0). Cost is estimated from public list prices.

Finding 1 — on clean invoices, OCR-then-text wins. Tesseract reads crisp, computer-generated invoices cleanly, and the text model extracts them accurately — at the lowest cost and latency. A small VLM reading the image directly makes more line-item errors (it misreads decimals — 3.17 → 31.17 — and shifts columns). This nuances our earlier "VLM over OCR" decision: that argument holds for degraded/complex layouts, not clean digital invoices.
Finding 2 — the VLM gap was the model, not the approach. Swapping gemma for Qwen3-VL-8B lifted line-item F1 from 0.84 to 0.98 and read the hardest invoice (Uber) perfectly. So a capable VLM can match OCR on line items — it just still trails on a couple of header fields here, at ~2.6× the cost and 2× the latency. (Qwen-30B couldn't be benchmarked — the HF free-credit tier was exhausted mid-run; not a quality result.)

Experiment 2 — the routing ablation (clean and degraded)

The real question isn't "OCR or VLM" globally — it's which per document. We built a mixed set (each invoice in clean and a synthesized degraded form — half-resolution, blur, rotation, JPEG artifacts) and routed on a cheap signal: Tesseract's mean per-word OCR confidence. Low confidence ⇒ the image is degraded ⇒ send it to the VLM.

The confidence signal worked cleanly: clean docs scored ~90, and the small-print Uber receipt collapsed to 33 when degraded (so the router sent only it to the VLM). Larger-print invoices (WeWork, Cargo) stayed readable even when degraded — exactly the per-document discrimination we want.

Finding 3 — the router beats both pure strategies. It keeps OCR's accuracy on clean docs, rescues the one degraded doc OCR can't read, and does it at near-OCR cost — calling the VLM only 1 in 6 times. That's the principle: cheap by default, big guns only where they pay off. (Both paths used free gemma here; putting the stronger Qwen3-VL on the degraded branch lifts the degraded number further — the escalation tier.)

What this changed in our decisions

Before (a-priori)After (evidence-based)
"Classification-guided VLM over OCR" — read everything with the vision model."Route per document: OCR for clean (cheaper + more accurate there), VLM for degraded scans where OCR fails — decided by a cheap OCR-confidence signal."
The VLM is the default reader.The VLM is the escalation reader; OCR-then-text is the cheap default. A premium VLM is reserved for the degraded/low-confidence tail.
Reader choice argued from first principles.Reader choice measured on a gold set, and re-measurable on the firm's real mix.

The conclusions are provisional by design; the measurement loop is the deliverable.
